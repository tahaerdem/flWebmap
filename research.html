A Long History of
Devastating Earthquakes

The past quakes are hardly a first for the country, as Turkey has a long history of dealing with similar disasters. Despite its relatively modest footprint, it sits on the intersection of the Eurasian, African, and Arabian tectonic plates, making it one of the most seismically active regions in the world.

CHAPTER 02

The North Anatolian Fault

The North Anatolian fault is a major strike-slip fault zone that stretches over 1200 kilometers across northern Turkey.

Over the past century, seismologists have observed that the earthquakes have been occurring sequentially from east to west, with each major event triggering the next one further westward along the North Anatolian fault line. [1]

This fault has experienced numerous devastating earthquakes, such as the 1939 Erzincan earthquake, which resulted in over 32,000 fatalities and more than 100,000 injuries. Since then, there have been seven additional M7.0+ earthquakes, most recent of these being the 7.6-magnitude Golcuk-Kocaeli earthquake in 1999, which occurred very close to Istanbul, causing over 17,100 deaths and displacing 250,000 people. [2] This event has served as a catalyst for Turkey, shortly after brought with it widespread changes in the country's government, approach to disaster management, urban planning, construction codes, and public awareness about seismic risks.

The February 6 earthquakes raised significant concerns about the potential for a major seismic event near Istanbul. The East Anatolian Fault (EAF) that the sequence has occured directly neighbors the east-end of the North Anatolian Fault (NAF). Due to the close proximity of these fault lines, recent events may trigger tectonic activity in the neighboring fault by transferring stress from one to the other. Given the historical pattern of westward migration of major earthquakes along the NAF, seismologists believe that this westward progression is likely to continue, and the next major event could potentially occur near the city of Istanbul, posing an immense risk to the lives and infrastructure of this densely populated region.

A Century of Earthquakes
Along the North Anatolian Fault

Over the past century, eight +M7 earthquakes occurred along the North Anatolian Fault Line. Each happening further west of the one preceding it.

Experts are concerned that the February 6 Earthquakes might induce a future earthquake along the NAF near or under Istanbul.

The most recent major tectonic event along the North Anatolian Fault, the 1999 Izmit-Gölcük Earthquake has served as a catalyst for the country, causing widespread changes in the government, and brough new approaches to disaster management, urban planning, construction regulations, and raised the public's awareness about seismic risks.

CHAPTER 03

1999 Golcuk Earthquake

The most recent major tectonic event along the North Anatolian Fault, the 1999 Izmit-Gölcük Earthquake, struck on August 17 at 3:01 AM local time. With a magnitude of 7.6 and an epicenter just 90 kilometers east of Istanbul, this 37-second tremor claimed over 17,000 lives and displaced 500,000 others. As Turkey’s most destructive earthquake until the February 2023 events, it exposed critical vulnerabilities in the country’s disaster preparedness.

The incumbent government’s organizational failure and inability to respond effectively, combined with a long-standing financial crisis, resulted in them receiving less than 10% of the vote in the 2002 elections. This paved the way for the rise of the AKP, still the ruling party since the elections.

Following the elections, the newly-elected AKP rapidly began working towards their key commitments. They made the “Earthquake Tax” permanent, a tax initially implemented in November 1999 under the Ecevit government to generate funds for disaster preparedness and relief efforts. While many of these taxes were later repealed in the following years, AKP government has ruled that the Special Communication Tax was to be made permanent and adjusted over the years, such as during the COVID-19 pandemic when it was increased from 7.5% to 10%. According to TUSIAD, the government has since collected more than 88 Billion Turkish liras between 2000-2022.

In addition to financial measures, another crucial promise was to enforce and expand stricter construction codes, particularly the 2001 Decree on Construction and Building Inspection, to ensure new buildings could withstand seismic activity, thereby reducing the risk of future catastrophes. This, along with multiple urban renewal projects aimed at replacing old vulnerable structures with more resilient ones in high density urban areas such as Istanbul, was meant to uplift the urban centers of the country to the next century.

Lastly, the Disaster and Emergency Management Authority (AFAD) was created in 2009, to coordinate and improve disaster management and civil protection nationwide. The country did not have a state-run emergency response organization prior to this, which marked a significant shift in Turkey’s disaster management approach, with now a heavy emphasis on pre-disaster planning and risk reduction.

Since the 2002 elections, the AKP has remained in power, largely due to initiatives that many believed had transformed Turkey from a run-down, old country to modern standards, with new highways, residences, and other infrastructure improvements. However, the February 6 earthquakes have led to widespread disillusionment. The devastation revealed that many of the government’s commitments were not fulfilled, exposing significant inadequacies and corruption in reconstruction and disaster preparedness efforts.

The Big Istanbul Earthquake

The devastating earthquake in February has heightened anxiety among Istanbul’s residents about the inevitable arrival of “the big one.” A city of 16 million (20 million, including undocumented residents according to the experts) living in highly dense neighborhoods with apartment buildings of five stories or taller, many of which were built before the 1999 quake thus not up to the latest building codes. These older structures, more than 625,000 of them, are particularly vulnerable, says Mehmet Özhaseki, Minister of Environment and Urbanism citing studies conducted by both the city and Ankara government.

Since then, the Municipality has conducted various studies that tries to simulate the extent of damage (both buildings, people, and infrastructure) and shared it on its Open Data portal in the form a CSV document. The above map is an interactive visualization of this data, showing the number of damaged buildings, injuries, death toll, and shelter availability per neighborhood. This study allows us to have an overall idea by ZIP code, yet it lack the granularity of a high-resolution map at the building level - which is critical, as the extent of damage varies per structure dramatically.

To tackle this, the City has also launched a Rapid Scan program, that allows residents to opt in for a less in-depth yet faster structural scanning program. However, skyrocketing property prices prevent many from moving out, even if they fear their building is unsafe. Efforts by the municipality to assess earthquake resilience have met resistance, with 65% of residents refusing rapid scanning due to concerns over potential findings. Imamoglu adds: "Even with the fast scan program, many residents do not want to opt in simply because they cannot afford to relocate." Despite the political challenges, authorities have been able to kickstart several housing projects totaling up to an impressive 300,000 units. Yet even such high numbers cannot meet the ever-increasing need for affordable housing that's exacerbated by the recent earthquakes and an ongoing economic crisis.

The situation becomes even more dire as the wait time for structural testing can stretch up to 21 years at the current rate of 120 buildings per day. With the North Anatolian Fault indicating an imminent earthquake, local architects, engineers, and students have initiated alternative methods for identifying structural issues. Using makeshift lists of easily identifiable deficiencies, these grassroots movements on social media have become highly popular among the public with posts sparking conversations and sharing of information at the scale of millions in helping people make crucial decisions involving their safety. This and many other initiatives has also shown that the solution to Istanbul's testing problem had to be more agile yet granular, and easily scalable.

The aftermath of the recent quakes has alarmed Istanbul Metropolitan Municipality, which has launched various action plans to ensure the safety of the city’s existing building stock by conducting free on-site structural tests, expediting urban renewal projects, and offering financial support and temporary housing for those who live in risky buildings. However, the newly-elected mayor Ekrem Imamoglu states that the increased demand to structural tests and other programs have exhausted the city’s limits.

The above map shows that only a small percentage of the city have been able to be tested since 2019, pointing out to a need for an alternative, faster approach. Another significant challenge dates back to 1940s when the country encouraged rural-to-urban migration to solve the increasingly pressing working shortage as Istanbul became an important global production center. Due to the lack of resources and interest in expanding the city for planned low-income housing neighborhoods, the governments have allowed workers to build their own housing on various parts of the city. The term “gecekondu” (which literally means ‘built overnight’) is used today to define the present urban patches and neighborhoods that got swallowed by the fast-sprawling city.

Common Reasons Behind
Structural Failures in the Region

Many videos and photos like the one above from the moment of collapse all hint at a common type of structural failure: First, the ground floor suddenly collapses, followed by all the floors above shortly after, pancaking on top of each other layer by layer in just a matter of seconds.


These “pancake collapses” are recognized as the primary cause of devastating consequences resulting from earthquakes in the region, such as the 1999 Golcuk earthquake, as reported by the infrastructure assessment team sent to the area. The factors leading to this phenomenon have been researched extensively and can be narrowed down to four primary construction/design shortcomings.



1. Soft Story Buildings

Soft story buildings are multiple story buildings that has a ground floor that has large windows, wide openings, and large open commercial shopfronts where a shear wall or vertical support element would typically be needed to ensure that the structural stability. Especially in earthquake-prone areas such as Turkey, at least 30% of the ground floor area must be dedicated to structural elements to ensure that the structural system could withstand the stress of the above floors. Soft story buildings are highly common in the region, with many multi-story apartment buildings accommodate commercial floor space on the ground floor, which is allowed by mixed-used zoning practices. The 1999 quake has led to an updated building code that prohibited such structures, many developers did not feel pressed to follow the regulations due to the lack of a strictly enforced control mechanism.


2. Heavy Overhangs

Another common practice is excessive cantilevering above the ground floor, as this allows developers to go beyond the site land usage limitations to maximize profits over extra square footage. Although cantilevering structures are a common practice in architectural design, combined with other malpractices such as soft story building designs, or using lower-quality building materials significantly increases the structural stress on the ground-level structural system. This may lead to a structural failure in the moment of an earthquake, even when the building may be able to withstand this load under normal conditions.


3. Misaligned Floor Slabs

Another common practice is excessive cantilevering above the ground floor, as this allows developers to go beyond the site land usage limitations to maximize profits over extra square footage. 4. Short Columns, Missing Beams and Other Structural Alterations

Structural alterations such as removing vertical supporting elements on the ground floor to open up space for commercial activities is another common practice many building owners employ to maximize profitability. Although there are multiple laws and regulations strictly prohibiting this, many buildings that has such alterations go unnoticed as it’s mostly done in the inside of the buildings.


5. Usage of Prohibited Materials and Techniques

The 1999 Earthquake has shown that the usage of certain construction materials, such as “sea-sand mixed cement” or rebars with no ribs, can be a significant risk factor during a earthquake. Although most of these materials cannot be identified without a structural test, some easy-to-spot visual hints can offer useful information. Many locals are advised to look for sea shells on the surface of the concrete to identify whether the cement is mixed with sea-sand.
